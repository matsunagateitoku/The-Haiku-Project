Saitō Sanki (1900–1962) was a haiku poet known for a humanist sensibility that set him apart from both the aesthetic conservatism of the Hototogisu school and the formal radicalism of the free-verse movement. Born in Tokyo, he worked as a dentist while composing haiku, and his verses often convey a compassionate attention to ordinary human life alongside imagery from the natural world. He is associated with the "human exploration" tendency in mid-twentieth century haiku.
