Poet
Matsuo Bashō
松尾芭蕉 1644 – 1694
Shōmon

Matsuo Bashō (1644–1694) is the most celebrated poet in the haiku tradition and the figure most responsible for transforming haikai no renga from a game of wit into a serious literary art. Born Matsuo Kinsaku in Ueno, Iga Province (present-day Mie Prefecture), he entered the household of a local lord as a page and developed an early love of poetry under the lord's son, who was himself a haikai enthusiast. After his patron died in 1666, Bashō drifted to Kyoto and eventually to Edo, where he established himself as a haikai teacher and began gathering disciples.

In the 1680s, Bashō underwent a decisive artistic transformation. Under the influence of Zen practice and immersion in classical Chinese poetry — especially the Tang poets Li Bai and Du Fu — he developed the aesthetic that would define his mature work: a quality he called sabi, a sense of solitude and transience suffused with quiet acceptance, and the deeper concept of wabi, finding beauty in austere simplicity. His famous frog poem — furuike ya, kawazu tobikomu, mizu no oto — composed around 1686, is often taken as the emblem of this new style: the shock of a concrete, unremarkable event opening onto vast stillness.

Travel became the central act of his poetic life. Between 1684 and his death, Bashō made a series of long journeys on foot through Japan, recording them in prose-and-verse travel diaries that are among the masterworks of Japanese literature. The greatest of these, Oku no Hosomichi (Narrow Road to the Deep North, 1689), describes a five-month journey through the remote northern provinces and is remarkable for the precision of its imagery and the depth of feeling compressed into its linked prose and haiku sequences.

He died in Osaka in 1694, on the road once more, surrounded by his disciples. His influence on all subsequent haiku has been total: every major poet since has defined their practice in relation to his, whether by discipleship, revision, or revolt.
