Takahama Kyoshi (1874–1959) was Masaoka Shiki's disciple and the dominant force in Japanese haiku for much of the twentieth century. Born Ikeuchi Kyoshi in Matsuyama, Ehime, he joined Shiki's circle as a young man and took over the editorship of the influential magazine Hototogisu after Shiki's death in 1902. He maintained Shiki's doctrine of shasei (sketching from life) while insisting on the retention of seasonal words (kigo) and the traditional 5-7-5 syllable structure — a conservative position that defined mainstream haiku practice for decades. Through Hototogisu, which he edited until old age, he trained and published the next several generations of major haiku poets, including Nakamura Teijo, Hoshino Tatsuko, and Sugita Hisajo. He died at eighty-five, having overseen Japanese haiku for more than fifty years.
