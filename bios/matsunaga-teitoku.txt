Matsunaga Teitoku (1571–1653) was the founding figure of the Teimon school and the poet most responsible for establishing haiku — then still called haikai no renga — as a distinct literary art with its own conventions, vocabulary, and audience. Born in Kyoto, he received a rigorous classical education and became a distinguished scholar of waka and renga, studying under the great classicist Nakanoin Michikatu. This classical grounding gave Teitoku an authority and prestige that no previous haikai poet had possessed.

Teitoku's contribution was essentially one of codification and elevation. Earlier haikai had been comic, vulgar, and anarchic — a deliberately low counterpart to the aristocratic renga tradition. Teitoku reformed it from within, insisting on learning and correctness while retaining the lightness that distinguished haikai from its graver cousin. He systematized the rules of composition, defined the seasonal vocabulary (kigo) that would remain central to haiku, and trained a generation of disciples who carried his methods across Japan. His school, the Teimon, dominated haikai in the first half of the seventeenth century.

His own poems tend toward wordplay and learned wit — the quality his contemporaries admired as tsukeai, the art of the apt connection. They are less admired today for their literary qualities than for their historical importance, and Teitoku himself is remembered more as an architect of the tradition than as a poet of individual genius. The reform movements that followed, from the Danrin school through Bashō and beyond, each defined themselves partly in reaction to Teimon conventions.

He was a prolific writer of prose as well as verse, and his memoirs and critical writings are an important source for the cultural history of early Edo-period literary life. He lived to the age of eighty-one, one of the longest-lived poets of his era, and remained productive until late in his life.
