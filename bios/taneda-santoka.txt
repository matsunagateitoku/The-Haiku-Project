Taneda Santōka (1882–1940) was a wandering Zen monk who wrote free-verse haiku about the road, rain, poverty, and sake. Born Taneda Shōichi in Yamaguchi Prefecture, he experienced a series of catastrophes — his mother's suicide when he was a child, his father's business failure, his own descent into alcoholism — before becoming a Sōtō Zen monk in 1925. Thereafter he wandered Japan on foot for most of the rest of his life, begging for food and lodging and composing haiku as he walked. His verses abandon the 5-7-5 form and typically omit the seasonal word; they are notable for their stark simplicity and the unmediated quality of their feeling. Along with Ozaki Hōsai, he is the central figure in the free-verse haiku tradition.
