Onitsura (1661–1738), whose full name was Uejima Onitsura, developed his haiku practice independently from Matsuo Bashō and arrived at similar insights through a different route. Born in Itami, Settsu Province (present-day Hyogo Prefecture), the son of a sake brewer, he began composing haiku as a child and eventually developed the concept of makoto — sincerity or truthfulness — as the central value of the art, arguing that verse not grounded in genuine experience was worthless. His work was largely overlooked during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and was rediscovered by scholars in the modern period, when his independent development of ideas close to Bashō's attracted renewed interest.
