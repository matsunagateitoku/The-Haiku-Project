Yamazaki Sōkan (c. 1465–c. 1553) is often identified as one of the earliest pioneers of the haiku form. He served at the imperial court in Kyoto before eventually withdrawing to Buddhist monastic life. He is closely associated with the anthology Inu Tsukubashū (c. 1530s), a collection of comic haikai verses whose earthy, often bawdy character was a deliberate contrast to the refinements of classical waka. His biographical details are imprecise, and his historical importance outweighs his reputation as an individual poet.
