Kawahigashi Hekigodō (1873–1937) was Masaoka Shiki's most prominent disciple and the leading figure in post-Shiki haiku reform. Born in Matsuyama, Ehime — the same city as Shiki — he became Shiki's close friend and literary collaborator. After Shiki's death in 1902, he argued for a haiku that moved beyond fixed seasonal references (kigo) and strict 5-7-5 syllable counts, eventually advocating for what he called "new haiku" grounded in direct experience rather than convention. This position put him in sustained conflict with Takahama Kyoshi, and the two represented the dominant poles of early twentieth century haiku debate. His own poems became progressively more experimental, and his influence faded in later decades as Kyoshi's conservatism came to dominate the mainstream.
