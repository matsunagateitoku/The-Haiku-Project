Poet
Kobayashi Issa
小林一茶 1763 – 1827
Edo

Kobayashi Issa (1763–1828) is the most beloved of the classical haiku poets, and the one whose life most visibly shadows his work. Born Kobayashi Yatarō in the mountain village of Kashiwabara in Shinano Province (present-day Nagano), he lost his mother at the age of three and endured a difficult childhood after his father remarried. At fourteen he was sent to Edo to make his own way, and spent the next two decades studying haikai, teaching, and traveling in poverty through the provinces. He adopted the pen name Issa — "one cup of tea" — sometime in the 1790s, and the name captures something essential about his aesthetic: brief, warm, slightly melancholy, and found in the ordinary.

Issa's haiku stand apart from those of Bashō and Buson in their radical democratization of subject matter and feeling. Where his predecessors inhabited a world of landscapes, seasons, and classical reference, Issa wrote about fleas, lice, snails, and flies — creatures at the very bottom of the natural order — with a tenderness that is never condescending and never sentimental. His identification with the small and the overlooked arose from lived experience: he was himself an outsider, chronically poor, not especially respected by the literary establishment of his day. His sympathy for creatures under threat feels personal because it was.

He returned to Kashiwabara in 1813 to claim his inheritance, a legal struggle that would last years. In the short middle period of his life he married, had children, and knew brief happiness; all five of his children died in infancy, and his wife died young. His diary Oraga Haru (The Year of My Life, 1819) is one of the great documents of Japanese personal literature — a prose-and-verse record of the year his daughter Sato died, written with grief that is all the more devastating for its quietness.

He died in 1828 in a fire that destroyed most of his house, aged sixty-five. He had composed roughly twenty thousand haiku — more than any other major poet — and left a body of work in which smallness, suffering, and a kind of fierce tenderness are inseparable.
