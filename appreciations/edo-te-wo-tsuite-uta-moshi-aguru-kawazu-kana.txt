The idea of associating a frog’s croaking with songs sung by people dates back to the kana preface to the Kokinshū in which Ki no Tsurayuki states, “As warblers sing amongst the blossoms and as frogs sing in the water, all living creatures compose songs.” Sōkan combined this popular topic with the keen observation that the way a frog sits with its hands out in front of itself is reminiscent of the pose of a poet bowing in front of a lofty audience, reciting his poem.  This method of combining an original visual image with a classical topic that traditionally was associated with sound is typical of haikai. The moderate, innocuous humor is characteristic of Sōkan.
