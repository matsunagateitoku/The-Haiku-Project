Poet
Yosa Buson
与謝蕪村 1716 – 1784
Edo

Yosa Buson (1716–1784) was a painter and poet who brought to haiku an aesthetic sensibility unlike any of his predecessors — one rooted in the visual arts and in a deep, imaginative engagement with classical Chinese culture. Born near Osaka, he moved to Edo as a young man and studied haikai under a disciple of Bashō, then spent years wandering the provinces in the manner of his great predecessor before eventually settling in Kyoto, where he would spend the rest of his life. He became one of the foremost painters of the Nanga (literati) school, and his paintings and his haiku share a quality of refined lyricism and deliberate pictorial composition.

Where Bashō cultivated sabi — a poetry of solitude, impermanence, and spiritual depth — Buson's haiku are painterly, sensuous, and often drenched in light and color. He was drawn to the textures of the visible world: the precise blue of a spring sky, lamplight on a white wall, the sound of a temple bell traveling across cold water. His poems create scenes with the economy and luminosity of fine brushwork. He was also deeply learned in Chinese poetry, and a quality of elegant nostalgia — a longing for a classical world he could only imagine — runs through much of his best work.

Buson was largely neglected in the century after his death, overshadowed by the towering reputation of Bashō and, later, the more accessible pathos of Issa. It was Masaoka Shiki who rescued him, arguing in the 1890s that Buson's painterly concreteness made him in some respects a more useful model for modern haiku than Bashō's inward spirituality. Shiki's rehabilitation stuck: Buson is now regarded, alongside Bashō and Issa, as one of the three great classical haiku poets.

He died in Kyoto at the age of sixty-seven, having spent his final years in the company of admiring students and in the prolific production of both paintings and poems. His dual achievement as a major figure in two distinct arts remains unmatched in Japanese literary history.
