Masaoka Shiki (1867–1902) was the dominant force in the reform of haiku at the end of the nineteenth century. Born Masaoka Tsunenori in Matsuyama, Ehime Prefecture, he adopted the pen name Shiki — the Japanese name for the hototogisu, the lesser cuckoo — after coughing blood in 1889, an early sign of the tuberculosis that would define and finally end his life.

Shiki studied at Tokyo Imperial University, where he became interested in literature and began writing haiku in the tradition of Bashō. He soon grew dissatisfied with what he saw as the stagnation of the haiku world, dominated by the Teimon and Danrin schools and their taste for verbal wit over observed reality. His response was shasei — "sketching from life" — a doctrine borrowed from Western plein-air painting that demanded the poet render the visible world with precision and freshness, without inherited convention.

His critical writings were as consequential as his poems. In a series of essays beginning in 1892, he rehabilitated the reputation of Yosa Buson, long overshadowed by Bashō, arguing that Buson's painterly concreteness was a model for modern haiku. He also subjected Bashō to critical scrutiny, judging many of his most celebrated poems by the standard of shasei and finding them deficient — a provocation that scandalized the haiku establishment.

From 1897, Shiki was bedridden. Spinal tuberculosis had left him unable to move, and his suffering was severe and largely unmedicated. He continued to write from his sickroom in Tokyo, editing his journal Hototogisu, corresponding with disciples including Takahama Kyoshi and Kawahigashi Hekigodō, and composing haiku and tanka until the end. His late sequence Byōshō Rokushaku — "A Sickbed Six Feet Square" — is among the most searching documents in the literature of illness.

He died on September 19, 1902, at the age of thirty-four, three days after composing his famous hechima deathbed poems. In thirty-four years and roughly a decade of productive work, he had transformed haiku from a parlor game into a modern literary form.
