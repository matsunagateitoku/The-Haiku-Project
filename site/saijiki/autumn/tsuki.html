Saijiki · Autumn
moon
月 tsuki
Autumn Celestial
Essay

The moon is the great presiding image of classical Japanese poetry. It enters haiku not as spectacle but as presence — something already there when the poem begins, already known, requiring no introduction. Autumn is its season because the autumn sky is clearest, the nights longest, and the harvest moon (meigetsu) at its fullest precisely when the year begins its long decline. In the haiku tradition the moon is never merely decorative. It is a measure: of loneliness, of the passage of time, of the strange fact that the same light falls on everyone. Bashō's most celebrated moon poems work by displacement — the moon appears obliquely, through water, through shadow, through its effect on the poet rather than its appearance in the sky. Later poets, especially in the modern period, push against the moon's weight of convention, finding it again in unexpected places: a hospital window, a city street, the palm of an upturned hand. The word tsuki alone carries the season. No further indication is needed.
