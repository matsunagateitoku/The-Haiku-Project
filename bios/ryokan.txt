Ryōkan (1758–1831), whose formal name was Ryōkan Taigu ("Great Fool"), was a Sōtō Zen monk and poet who spent most of his adult life as a hermit on Mount Kugami in Echigo Province (present-day Niigata). Born the eldest son of a village headman in Izumozaki, he entered monastic life young and trained for years at Entsū-ji in Tamashima before returning to his home province. He lived in extreme simplicity — begging for food, spending his days in meditation, calligraphy, and poetry, and playing with local children, for whom he was a beloved figure. He wrote in both haiku and waka, and his poems are valued for their directness, warmth, and lack of literary pretension. His work was not widely published during his lifetime but found a large and lasting audience in subsequent generations.
